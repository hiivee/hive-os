# Mode A — Opening (Empowerment Promise + First 60 Seconds)

> Source: Patrick Winston, *How to Speak*, MIT OCW. Winston's central claim: the first 60 seconds either earn the next 60 minutes, or they don't.

---

## Intake required

From `assets/intake-questions.md`: questions 1–4 (universal) + 5–6 (Mode A add-ons).

Do not proceed until you have all six. The empowerment promise is impossible to write without #5 (single most valuable takeaway).

---

## Steps

### Step 1 — Identify the single most valuable takeaway
The audience will remember at most one outcome. Pick the highest-value, most concrete one. If the user lists multiple, force a ranking and pick #1.

### Step 2 — Convert it into an empowerment promise
A correct empowerment promise has three properties:

1. **Specific** — names a concrete capability, not a topic. (Topic: "machine learning." Capability: "diagnose which model is failing on production traffic and why.")
2. **Outcome-driven** — phrased as a *result the audience walks away with*, not as a description of what the speaker will cover.
3. **Time-anchored** — explicitly states *by the end of [this hour / these 20 minutes / this talk]*. The deadline creates psychological commitment.

**Template:**
> "By the end of the next [N] minutes, you will [be able to / understand / have a framework for] [specific capability], so that [meaningful downstream consequence]."

### Step 3 — Design the first 60 seconds
Allocate ~150 spoken words across three beats:

| Beat | Words | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Promise** (≈25 words) | The empowerment promise itself, delivered as the very first sentence after greeting. | Earns the next 60 minutes. |
| **Context** (≈75 words) | Why this matters now. A concrete moment, number, or stake the audience already feels. Winston's "shock" technique fits here — an unprecedented number or counterintuitive fact. | Makes the promise feel urgent rather than academic. |
| **Why this speaker, why now** (≈50 words) | A one-line credibility marker (without becoming a bio dump) and the reason this is the right room and the right moment. | Establishes you have standing to deliver on the promise. |

### Step 4 — Cut everything that does not serve the promise
Apply the cut list (see Rules below). Flag each violation found in the user's draft, and either delete or rewrite.

### Step 5 — Deliver the four-part output (see Output format)

---

## Rules — non-negotiable

- **No jokes in the opening.** The audience is still settling — laptops closing, phones away. Humor lands later, after rapport.
- **No "thank you for having me."** Forgettable. Burns the most valuable real estate of the talk.
- **No apologies.** ("Sorry, I just flew in…" / "I had to throw this together…") Winston: every apology in the opening compounds. The audience hears "this will be bad."
- **No agenda slide as opener.** "Today I'll cover three things" is throat-clearing. The promise is the agenda.
- **No bio dump.** Credibility is one line, not a paragraph.
- **No vague promise.** "You'll learn about X" fails. Promise must be a capability the audience leaves with.
- **No promise without a deadline.** "By the end of this talk" is the minimum. Better: "by the end of the next 20 minutes."
- **First 60 seconds must earn the next 60 minutes.** If a sentence does not advance the promise, context, or credibility, cut it.

---

## Output format

Deliver exactly these four sections, in this order:

### 1. Empowerment Promise
A single sentence (≤30 words) following the template above.

### 2. First 60 Seconds (full script)
The verbatim opening the user will speak — promise, context, why-this-now — totalling ~150 words. Format as a single paragraph or three labelled mini-paragraphs.

### 3. What to Cut
Bullet list of every element from the user's current opening (or planned opening) that violates a rule. For each: name the violation and give the one-line fix.

### 4. Delivery Notes
Three or four short notes on how to deliver the opening — pacing, where to stand, what to do with hands or slides during the first 60 seconds. Winston-grounded only (e.g. "stand close to the screen if a slide is up; do not retreat to the lectern").

---

## Anti-patterns (flag if seen in the user's draft)

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|--------------|--------------|-----|
| "I'm excited to be here…" | Self-focused, audience does not care. | Open on the promise. |
| "A funny thing happened on the way…" | Joke before rapport. | Move humor to minute 5+. |
| "Let me start with a quote from [famous person]." | Borrowed authority delays your own. | Use the quote in body, not opening. |
| "Quick agenda: first I'll cover, then…" | The promise *is* the agenda. | Replace with the promise. |
| "There's a lot to cover so let's dive in." | Signals overload, not value. | Replace with the deadline-anchored promise. |
| "I won't bore you with the details…" | Pre-apologising. Audience now expects boredom. | Cut entirely. |

## Worked mini-example

**User's draft opening:**
> "Hi everyone, thanks so much for having me. I'm Priya, I lead the platform team at Acme. Today I want to talk about our migration to event-driven architecture. There's a lot to cover, so let's dive in."

**Violations:** thank-you opener, bio dump, vague topic-not-promise, "lot to cover" throat-clearing.

**Rewrite (Empowerment Promise + first 60 seconds):**
> "By the end of the next 20 minutes, you will know exactly which three signals tell you a system is ready for event-driven migration — and which two will mislead you into starting too early. Last quarter, four teams in this company started that migration; one finished on time, three are still in flight, and the difference came down to those five signals. I led one of the teams that finished. I'm going to walk you through the signal checklist, the two traps, and a one-page decision framework you can apply to your own service before you leave this room."

## Hand-off

After delivering, offer Mode C (Structure) so the strong opening leads into a structured talk that delivers on the promise. Phrase the offer as a single short question.
